2. Executive Summary

NECX is a coordinated platform serving victims and survivors of crime alongside the agencies and advocates who help them. A single unified backend serves four distinct clients — the NECX Admin console (operated by NECX Inc), the VS360 web dashboard (agency staff), and the Workspace and Victim mobile applications. Because these audiences range from platform super-administrators who can see every tenant to individual victims who must only ever see their own case, access control is not a peripheral concern — it is the foundation on which the platform's safety, privacy, and multi-tenancy guarantees rest.

This document specifies the Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) and User Management capability of NECX. It defines the identity model that distinguishes platform/staff users (users) from victim end-users (app_users), the three issuer types (NECX, VSMS, APP) that classify every authenticated caller, and the two enforcement layers that gate every protected request: a coarse context gate (allowOnly) that segregates traffic by client, and a fine-grained module→action permission matrix evaluated per role. It documents both permission catalogs in full — the NECX platform modules map and the agency vs360Modules map — as well as the SUPER_ADMIN bypass, role creation and permission assignment, agency user provisioning with plan-driven seat limits, and the three consistent frontend enforcement layers (route guard, navigation gating, and in-page action checks).

RBAC at NECX is deliberately cascading: the platform defines the roles and plans that agencies consume; each agency is provisioned with a plan that caps which roles and how many seats it may use; and agency administrators then assign those roles to their own staff. This model lets NECX Inc govern the platform centrally while allowing each tenant to self-administer within safe, contractually bounded limits.

Correct access control directly protects vulnerable people. A permission leak could expose a survivor's location, case notes, or shelter placement to an unauthorized party. Accordingly, this PRD treats least-privilege defaults, complete auditability, and defense-in-depth (never trusting the client alone) as first-class, non-negotiable requirements.

3. Product Overview & Vision

Problem Statement

The NECX platform hosts many independent agencies (tenants) on shared infrastructure, each with staff of varying seniority, plus NECX Inc's own platform operators, plus the victims themselves. These populations must be strictly isolated from one another and internally stratified: a caseworker at Agency A must never see Agency B's data; a junior advocate may log cases but not manage roles; a victim may only see their own services. Without a rigorous, centrally governed access-control system, the platform cannot uphold the confidentiality that victim services demand, cannot enforce commercial plan limits, and cannot produce the audit trail required for grant and compliance reporting.

Product Vision

RBAC & User Management provides a single, coherent authorization model spanning all four clients. Access decisions are driven by two composable layers — a client-context gate and a fine-grained permission map — that are evaluated identically on the backend for every request and mirrored on the frontend for responsive UX. Platform administrators author roles and plans once; those definitions cascade down to tenants; and every agency self-administers its own staff within the seats and roles its plan allows. Every mutation is written to an immutable audit log.

Where This Sits in the NECX Ecosystem

RBAC is a cross-cutting core service of the unified backend (necx-backend_v2). It underpins:

  • NECX Admin — where platform super-admins define roles for both the Admin and VS360 contexts, bundle roles into plans, provision agencies with seat counts, and create/manage NECX, VSMS, and App users.
  • VS360 web — where agency administrators create agency staff, assign plan-permitted roles, and where every page, nav item, and action button is gated by the user's permission map.
  • Workspace & Victim mobile apps — where victim (APP) users authenticate without a context gate (auth-only) and operate strictly within their own data.

RBAC consumes the platform's authentication service (which resolves each caller into req.issuer) and is consumed by every feature module (claims, shelters, hotline, notes, communities, reports, and so on), each of which declares the context and permission it requires.

4. Goals, Objectives & Success Metrics

Goals

  • Enforce strict tenant isolation — a VS360 user only ever sees and edits data belonging to their own agency (serviceCenterInfo.id).
  • Provide fine-grained, least-privilege access — every sensitive action is individually gated by a module→action permission flag, not by broad role names alone.
  • Centralize governance, distribute administration — NECX Inc defines roles/plans; agencies self-administer staff within plan limits.
  • Enforce defense-in-depth — the backend is the source of truth for every access decision; the frontend mirrors permissions only for UX and is never trusted alone.
  • Guarantee complete auditability — every create/update/delete on identity, role, and permission objects writes an ActionLogs row.
  • Enforce commercial constraints — plans cap the roles an agency may assign and the number of seats it may consume.

5. Scope

5.1 In Scope

  • The identity model: users vs app_users, the vsms_users agency-membership join, and the three issuer types (NECX, VSMS, APP).
  • The req.issuer shape (id, uid, type, roleInfo with context + permission, and serviceCenterInfo).
  • The context gate (allowOnly) and its allow-list values (ADMIN, VS360), including multi-context routes.
  • The complete NECX platform permission catalog (modules) and agency permission catalog (vs360Modules), documented as full tables.
  • The fine-grained permission middleware (module.action gate) evaluated in services.
  • The SUPER_ADMIN bypass semantics on both backend and frontend.
  • Roles CRUD (create, read, update, delete) with permission-JSON authoring, in both the ADMIN and VS360 contexts.
  • Assigning permissions to roles and assigning roles to users.
  • Provisioning agency (VSMS) users — inline from agency detail and via the VS360 Users tab and VS360 "Staff Management".
  • Plan-driven role catalogs (GET /roles/plan/:id) and seat limits (numberOfUsers).
  • Frontend enforcement: ProtectedPage route guard, LeftSideNavbar/nav gating, and in-page hasPermission/isSuperAdmin checks.
  • User activation/deactivation (archival) and onboarding-email delivery.
  • Auditing of identity/role/permission mutations via ActionLogs.

5.2 Out of Scope

  • Authentication mechanics themselves (OTP login, password reset, login codes, PIN/step-up, token rotation) — covered by the Platform Core & Authentication PRD; referenced here only where they intersect user creation.
  • The internal business logic of feature modules gated by RBAC (claims, shelters, hotline, communities, reports) — covered by their respective PRDs.
  • Agency (tenant) provisioning end-to-end, hotline/Twilio provisioning, and plan billing mechanics — covered by the NECX Admin and Provisioning PRDs (referenced where relevant to seats/roles).
  • Victim self-registration flows on mobile beyond their app_user identity classification.
  • Transport-level response encryption and edge-proxy secrets (X-Netlify-Secret) — a platform concern, noted only as context.
ℹ Least privilege by default
New roles are authored with an explicit, opt-in permission map. A role grants nothing until an administrator toggles specific module→action switches, and the switches shown are constrained to what the agency's plan permits.

7. System Context & Architecture

Tech Stack

The RBAC system lives in the unified backend necx-backend_v2 (Node.js + Express, ESM; Objection/Knex over PostgreSQL; Socket.IO; Redis; BullMQ). It is consumed by three frontends: NECX Admin (React 18 + Vite + Mantine + Redux + TanStack Query), VS360 web (React 18 + Vite + Mantine + Redux + TanStack Query), and the mobile apps.

Request Model & Enforcement Chain

The backend uses a tri-router pattern: nearly every feature module exports adminRouter (/admin/*), webRouter (/web/*), and mobileRouter (/mobile/*), mounted under the API version prefix v2 (e.g. /v2/admin, /v2/web, /v2/mobile). The URL prefix declares the intended client. Every protected request flows through a middleware chain:

  1. Rate limiter (named limiters: standard, auth, authStrict, passwordReset).
  2. Auth middleware — validates the JWT and populates req.issuer.
  3. Context middleware — resolves roleInfo.context.
  4. allowOnly(...context) — the context gate (403 if the caller's context is absent or not allow-listed).
  5. Permission middleware — the fine-grained module.action gate (also enforced inside services).
  6. Validator (Joi) → controller → service → repository/model.

RBAC is therefore evaluated at two layers on every protected request, before any business logic runs.

Talking to the Backend

Both web clients use an Axios factory with interceptors that inject Authorization: Bearer <accessToken>, x-refresh-token, X-Netlify-Secret, and X-Environment; rotate the refresh token from the response header; decrypt AES-encrypted response bodies; and force a full logout on HTTP 401. The login response carries the user's roleInfo (with permission) plus the permission catalogs (modules / vs360Modules) used to render the role editor.

Tenancy

Multi-tenancy is enforced server-side: agency-scoped data is filtered by req.issuer.serviceCenterInfo.id, so a VS360 user only ever sees or edits their own agency. On the frontend, tenant context rides on the persisted auth.user object (serviceCenterInfo, roleInfo, vsmsUserInfo); socket rooms and RBAC checks derive scope from it. The vsms_users table joins a users row to a service_centers row to establish agency membership.

Real-Time

Socket.IO authenticates via a JWT in the connection handshake (auth: { token, refreshToken }), not headers, and joins per-user rooms (vs360-user-<uid>), the agency room (service-center-<id>), and global rooms. Access to socket-delivered data follows the same tenant scoping as REST.

8. Functional Requirements

8.1 Identity Model

NECX maintains two identity tables. users holds platform and agency staff (NECX Inc operators and agency caseworkers/administrators). app_users holds victims/survivors. Credentials live separately in user_credentials (bcrypt). Agency membership is established by vsms_users, which joins a users row to a service_centers (agency) row; vsms_user_service_categories scopes a staff member to specific service categories.

Every authenticated caller is resolved by the auth middleware into req.issuer, carrying id, uid, type, roleInfo (with context + permission), and — for staff — serviceCenterInfo. The type classifies the caller:

8.2 The Context Gate (allowOnly)

The coarse authorization layer is the context gate, implemented in src/middleware/allowOnly.js. It reads req.issuer.roleInfo.context and returns 403 if the value is absent or not in the route's allow-list. The allow-list values are ADMIN (NECX platform) and VS360 (agency). Mobile victim routes usually omit the guard entirely (auth-only). Some routes allow multiple contexts — for example the roles list is guarded allowOnly('VS360','ADMIN'), letting both platform admins and agency admins read/manage roles for their respective contexts.

IDRequirementPriority
FR-CTX-01Each protected route SHALL declare an allowOnly(...) allow-list of contexts; the gate SHALL 403 when the caller's roleInfo.context is absent or not listed.Must
FR-CTX-02The allow-list values SHALL be ADMIN and VS360.Must
FR-CTX-03Mobile victim (APP) routes SHALL be auth-only (no context gate) unless a specific route requires otherwise.Must
FR-CTX-04Routes intended for both platform and agency admins (e.g. roles) SHALL declare allowOnly('VS360','ADMIN').Must
FR-CTX-05The context gate SHALL run before the fine-grained permission middleware and before any controller/service logic.Must

8.3 Fine-Grained Permission Model

A role's permission object is a module → action → boolean map, defined in src/lib/constants/role-permissions.js and evaluated in services and permission-middleware.js. There are two permission templates: the NECX platform catalog (modules) and the agency catalog (vs360Modules). Roles (roles table) hold a name plus this permission JSON, scoped per agency/platform (serviceCenterId present for agency roles; system roles have none and are locked).

The frontend checks the same map via hasPermission(appUser, module, action), reading appUser.roleInfo.permission[module][action] (module hyphens normalized to underscores).

8.4 SUPER_ADMIN Bypass

The SUPER_ADMIN role short-circuits all fine-grained permission checks and returns access on every module/action. On the backend it bypasses the permission middleware/service checks; on the frontend, isSuperAdmin(roleName) normalizes the role name (strips non-alpha, lowercases) and, if it equals superadmin, makes hasPermission(...) return true everywhere. The Admin header always renders the "SUPER ADMIN" badge for such a user. Super-admin identity is recognized either via roleInfo.roleName or membership in the user's roles[] array.

The context gate still applies structurally (a super-admin operates within the ADMIN context), but the fine-grained layer is fully bypassed.

IDRequirementPriority
FR-SA-01A SUPER_ADMIN role SHALL bypass all fine-grained permission checks on both backend and frontend.Must
FR-SA-02Super-admin detection SHALL normalize the role name (non-alpha stripped, lowercased) and compare to superadmin; membership MAY also be resolved via the roles[] array.Must
FR-SA-03The bypass SHALL be restricted to the super-admin role; no other role SHALL receive an implicit bypass.Must
FR-SA-04The Admin UI SHALL display a "SUPER ADMIN" badge for super-admin users.Should

8.5 Roles CRUD & Permission Authoring

Roles are managed under Roles & Permissions (module role_management) in both Admin and VS360. In Admin, roles are authored for two target panels via roleContextPanels = { "VS360 Panel": "VS360", "Admin Panel": "ADMIN" }. The role editor (RolePermissionForm.jsx / RolePermissionsForm.jsx) loads the permission catalog — modules for the Admin panel, vs360Modules for the VS360 panel — and renders a vertical tab per module with a permission switch per action, plus Select-All / Deselect-All per module. 8.8 Plan-Driven Seat Limits & Role Catalogs

A plan (plans + plan_roles) is a named bundle of VS360-context roles plus caps. Agencies are assigned a plan at provisioning, which determines: (a) which roles their users can be given, via GET /roles/plan/:planId; and (b) a seat count derived from the agency's numberOfUsers ("1 Super Admin + N-1 users"). The Admin UI disables "Add User" once users.length >= numberOfUsers; the role editor and user forms show only plan-permitted roles/actions.

FR-PLAN-03An agency's seat count SHALL be numberOfUsers; user creation beyond it SHALL be blocked (UI disables "Add User"; backend rejects).Must

⚠ Seat enforcement must be server-side
The UI disables "Add User" at the seat cap, but the backend MUST independently reject over-seat creation. A UI-only check is insufficient because clients cannot be trusted.

8.9 Frontend Enforcement Layers

Both web clients centralize RBAC in src/utils/utils.js (hasPermission, isSuperAdmin, getHomeRoute), reading the persisted state.auth.user.roleInfo. There is no separate RBAC context/provider. Three consistent gating layers, backed by the same helpers, apply:

  1. Route guard (ProtectedPage.jsx) — checks isAuth first (redirect to login with state.from if not). Then: super-admin or isForAll route → allow; a route with an explicit permission (string or array) → require all listed permissions; else fall back to module-presence (hasPermission(user, module, "view", true)). Denied → renders <NoViewPermission/> (or null while the user loads).
  2. Navigation gating (LeftSideNavbar.jsx hasAccess() / Admin NavbarLinks.jsx) — mirrors the same logic (isForAll → explicit permission array → module presence). Hidden items simply do not render; isForAll items (e.g. Settings) always show.
  3. In-page action checks — components call hasPermission(...) / isSuperAdmin(...) directly to render/enable individual buttons and actions.

The permission map is hydrated from the login/verify response (modules / vs360Modules plus user.roleInfo); getHomeRoute(navItems, user) selects the first nav item the user can see as the post-login landing page.

FR-FE-01ProtectedPage SHALL gate every protected route on authentication and RBAC; explicit permission values SHALL require ALL listed permissions.Must

9. Detailed User & System Flows

9.1 Create a Role & Assign Permissions (Admin, VS360 context)

  1. A platform admin with role_management.add opens NECX Admin → /users → Roles and Permissions tab.
  2. They click Create and choose the target panel: VS360 Panel (context: "VS360") or Admin Panel (context: "ADMIN").
  3. The editor loads the permission catalog from Redux — vs360Modules for VS360, modules for Admin — populated at login, and renders a vertical tab per module.
  4. The admin enters a roleName and description, then toggles permission switches per module (using Select-All / Deselect-All as needed). The editor enforces dependencies — e.g. claims.view_all cannot be enabled unless claims.view is on.
  5. On save, the client sends POST /roles with { roleName, permission, description, context }.
  6. Backend: allowOnly('VS360','ADMIN') passes (admin context), role_management.add passes, the role row is written with the permission JSON, and an ActionLogs entry is recorded.
  7. A confirm/success toast fires and the roles cache (["roles-pageless"]) is invalidated so the new role appears in lists and user-assignment selectors.

Branch — agency admin creating a role in VS360: identical, except step 3 additionally calls GET /roles/plan/:planId and the editor shows only the actions the agency's plan grants; system roles (no serviceCenterId) are not editable.

9.2 Invite / Create a User & Assign a Role (VS360 agency admin)

  1. An agency admin with user.add opens VS360 → Staff Management → Users tab and clicks Add User. If the agency is at its seat cap (users.length >= numberOfUsers), the button is disabled.
  2. In UserForm, they select one or more roles (from GET /roles?pageLess, constrained to the plan), enter first/last name, email, phone, a temporary password, an OTP channel (Email/SMS), and service categories.
  3. On submit (behind a confirm modal), the client sends POST /users adding type:"VSMS", the agency serviceCenterId, and mapped roleIds/categoryIds.
  4. Backend: allowOnly('VS360') passes, user.add passes, the seat cap is re-checked server-side, the users + vsms_users rows are created, credentials stored (bcrypt), and an ActionLogs entry written.
  5. Optionally the admin triggers the onboarding email (POST /users/send-onboarding-email/:uid) delivering credentials/instructions.
  6. The new staff member signs in (email + temp password → OTP verification); the verify response carries their roleInfo.permission (union of assigned roles) and the permission catalog, hydrating their session.
  7. getHomeRoute lands them on the first nav item their roles permit.

Branch — platform admin creating a VS360 user in Admin: from the agency detail page ("Add User") or the VS360 Users tab, the admin selects the agency, roles are fetched via GET /roles/plan/:planId, categories/OTP channel are set, and POST /users with type:"VSMS" provisions the account. Seat cap and plan-role constraints apply identically.

9.3 A Permission-Gated Action Allowed End-to-End (frontend + backend)

Scenario: caseworker Priya deletes a case note (notes_center.delete).

  1. Priya's session was hydrated at login with roleInfo.permission including notes_center: { view: true, delete: true, ... }.
  2. Nav layer: LeftSideNavbar.hasAccess() shows the Notes item because notes_center is present in her map.
  3. Route layer: navigating to /notes, ProtectedPage confirms isAuth and the route's module presence/permission — allowed.
  4. Action layer: the note detail drawer renders the Delete button because hasPermission(user, "notes_center", "delete") returns true.
  5. Priya clicks Delete; the client sends DELETE /notes/:noteUid with her bearer token.
  6. Backend: rate-limiter → auth (req.issuer resolved) → context middleware → allowOnly('VS360') passes → permission middleware confirms notes_center.delete → the service deletes the note (scoped to her agency) → writes an ActionLogs entry.
  7. A success response is returned; the notes list cache is invalidated and the UI updates.

9.4 A Permission-Gated Action Denied End-to-End (frontend + backend)

Scenario: caseworker Priya lacks role_management.* and attempts to reach or act on Roles & Permissions.

  1. Nav layer: the Roles & Permissions nav/tab does not render because her map has no role_management module presence.
  2. Route/tab layer: if Priya deep-links to the roles view, ProtectedPage (or the tab's hasPermission(user, "role_management", "view") check) fails and renders <NoViewPermission/> instead of the content.
  3. Action layer: even if a stale/cached UI exposed a control, the Delete/Save buttons are gated by hasPermission(...) and do not render/enable.
  4. Backend (defense-in-depth): if a crafted request nonetheless reaches DELETE /roles/:id, the permission middleware evaluates role_management.delete against her map, finds it absent (and she is not SUPER_ADMIN), and returns 403. No mutation occurs.
  5. The client surfaces a friendly error toast via handleErrorResponse (403 → "not permitted").

Branch — cross-tenant attempt: if Priya's request targets another agency's resource, the server's tenant scoping (serviceCenterInfo.id filter) yields a not-found/403 regardless of her permission map — an agency user can never reach another agency's data.

9.5 Plan Seat Enforcement

  1. An agency is provisioned with a plan and numberOfUsers = N ("1 Super Admin + N-1 users").
  2. As the agency admin adds staff, the Users list reflects current headcount.
  3. When users.length >= numberOfUsers, the Add User button is disabled in both VS360 and Admin (agency detail page).
  4. If a client bypasses the disabled control and still sends POST /users, the backend independently re-checks the seat cap for that agency and rejects the creation with an error.
  5. To add more seats, a platform admin edits the agency's numberOfUsers (and/or plan) in Admin; the new cap then permits additional users.
  6. Every creation attempt (allowed or rejected) is auditable via ActionLogs.

9.6 SUPER_ADMIN Access

  1. Dana (NECX Inc) signs in; her roleInfo.roleName normalizes to superadmin (or she is a member of the super-admin roles[]).
  2. Frontend: isSuperAdmin(...) returns true, so hasPermission(...) returns true for every module/action — all nav items, routes, and buttons are available; the header shows the "SUPER ADMIN" badge.
  3. Backend: the permission middleware detects the super-admin role and bypasses all fine-grained checks; the allowOnly context gate still applies structurally (she operates in ADMIN).
  4. Dana can define roles for both panels, manage plans and agencies, and manage all three user categories.

9.7 Deactivate (Archive) a Staff User

  1. An admin with user.edit opens the Users list and selects a staff member.
  2. They toggle Deactivate (behind a confirm modal), sending PATCH /users/:uid with isArchived: true (or PATCH /users/update-status/:uid).
  3. Backend validates context + user.edit, flips the archive flag, and writes an ActionLogs entry.
  4. The archived user can no longer authenticate; existing sessions lose access on next token validation/refresh.
  5. Reactivation reverses the flag via the same endpoint.

13. Non-Functional Requirements

Security & Privacy (victim safety)

  • Defense-in-depth: every access decision is enforced server-side; the frontend mirrors permissions for UX only and is never the sole gate. A denied frontend still yields a 403 on the backend.
  • Least privilege: new roles grant nothing until explicitly toggled; editors expose only plan-permitted actions; the super-admin bypass is confined to the SUPER_ADMIN role.
  • Tenant isolation: agency-scoped data is filtered by serviceCenterInfo.id; cross-tenant access is structurally impossible for VS360 users, independent of their permission map.
  • Credential protection: passwords are bcrypt-hashed in user_credentials and never returned; OTP-based two-step login protects staff sign-in; sensitive victim data is protected by ownership scoping.
  • Transport security: API responses are AES-encrypted in non-dev environments; the edge proxy validates X-Netlify-Secret; 401 forces full client logout.

Auditability

  • Nearly all create/update/delete operations — including role and user mutations — write an ActionLogs row capturing action, referenceType, and actionDescription (source, ip, url, context), providing a complete, immutable audit trail for compliance and grant/PMT reporting.

Performance & Availability

  • Permission checks are in-memory map lookups on data carried in the session; they add negligible latency to the middleware chain.
  • Role/user list endpoints are paginated; the plan catalog (GET /roles/plan/:id) is fetched once per editor session and cached client-side (TanStack Query).
  • The permission map is hydrated at login and persisted client-side, so authorization requires no per-navigation round-trips.

Consistency & Correctness

  • Frontend and backend evaluate the same module→action semantics; the three frontend layers (route, nav, action) share the same helpers, preventing drift.
  • Action dependencies (e.g. claims.view_all ⇒ claims.view) are enforced in the editor to prevent inconsistent permission maps.

Localization & Accessibility

  • Staff web UIs are English; the permission model itself is language-neutral. Role/permission editors follow the platform's Mantine accessibility conventions (keyboard-operable switches, confirm modals).

Compliance

  • The audit trail and least-privilege posture support grant/PMT reporting obligations and general data-protection expectations for handling sensitive victim data.

14. Analytics, Reporting & Notifications

  • Onboarding notification: new-user creation can trigger an onboarding email (POST /users/send-onboarding-email/:uid) with credentials/instructions, delivered via the BullMQ email queue; OTP for first sign-in is delivered by email or SMS (Twilio) per the user's channel.
  • Seat-usage visibility: the Admin agency detail page surfaces current headcount vs. numberOfUsers, disabling "Add User" at the cap — an operational signal for plan upgrades.
  • Audit reporting: ActionLogs provides the substrate for administrative/compliance reporting on who created/edited/deleted which identities, roles, and permissions, with source/IP/URL/context metadata.
  • PostHog (web): product analytics identify the logged-in user (staging/beta/production only) and tag events with platform/version; RBAC events are not separately instrumented beyond standard page/action tracking.
  • No dedicated RBAC dashboard exists today (the Admin dashboard is a placeholder); seat and audit reporting are surfaced via the agency detail page and audit logs respectively.
